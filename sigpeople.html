Harriet Tubman

  • From working with her father in lumber, she obtained exposure to free black sailors in which she over heard them speaking about a secret railroad where lies people of homes, boats, and wagons awaiting for black fugitives to take them up north and then Canada — liberation. Using this information and after failing her first attempt with her two brothers due to fear, in 1849 she was able to successfully make it to Pennsylvania. With confirmation of the route, she went back in forth, bringing her family and other innocent slaves to the north and even further up to Canada.
  • In 1863, Harriet become known to be the first woman in U.S. history to host a military raid which resulted in freeing almost 700 salves in South Carolina.
  • the 13th amendment legally  abolished slavery in the United States, the 14th amendment expanded citizenship and 15th gave voting rights to black men. This did not stop Harriet from opening up new schools and hospitals via raising funds for enslaved peoples.
  • Beginning in 1888, Harriet’s fame for women equality and right to vote grew, with her speaking in many organizations and movements such as her appearance in 1896 in Washington D.C. at National Association of Colored Women.
  • After with he help of many supporters, Harriet Tubman was finally granted her veteran pension with $20/month. Since 2016, the American $20 shows an image of Harriet Tubman on the front of the bill
  • She passed at the age of 91 year old on March 10, 1913 with her final words of: “I go, to prepare a place for you.”
